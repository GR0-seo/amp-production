Is a Rowing Machine a Good Workout?

Dec 24, 2019

Is a Rowing Machine a Good Workout?

The humble rowing machine for quite some time found itself relegated in the gym graveyard but this unloved piece of workout equipment is experiencing a renaissance. Today, rowing has become increasingly popular for its incredible aerobic-boosting, muscle-strengthening and fat-burning qualities. In addition, the return has been spearheaded by the emergence of calorie-burning group rowing classes and commercial-grade high-quality indoor rower machines that give users the feel of rowing with a team on the water.

If you find yourself asking Is a rowing machine a good workout? then keep reading. In this article we will explore:

The muscles that are used

The three rowing motions

The benefits

What Muscles Are Used

There are over 650 skeletal muscles that make up the human body. Each muscle has an important role to your overall health such as working to allow your heart to pump blood efficiently and facilitate movement. With that being said, it goes without saying that strengthening your muscles is crucial to support a healthy lifestyle. Rowing is an excellent way to do this because studies support that it engages 86% of the muscles within the body, but what muscles are used exactly?

To know which muscles are being utilized, it is important to understand the three motions that are involved with rowing: The Catch, The Drive, and The Finish.

The Catch
The catch is the beginning of a rowing stroke. Your arms are fully extended, and your hands grip the handle. The seat is slid all the way forward with your knees bent into your chest while keeping your shins positioned down. The muscles worked during this motion are in your upper body and specifically include:

Triceps

Deltoids

Trapezius

Lower back

Abdominals

Hamstrings

Calves

The Drive
The second phase of the rowing stroke is known as the drive and is the thrust of the rowing workout. As you press against the foot pedals, drive yourself back almost fully extending your legs. Use your core and hip hinge to swing your body into an upright position. Engage your shoulders, arms and back to pull your arms towards your chest or sternum forming a 90% angle with the monorail. These steps should all be completed as one fluid motion. The muscles worked during this motion include:

Pectoralis muscles

Trapezius

Biceps and forearms

Deltoids

Latissimus dorsi

Abdominal

Glutes

Quadriceps

Hamstrings

Calves

The Finish
During the finish, use your core muscles to stabilize your body as you hinge slightly backward at the hips. Using that momentum, fully extend your legs and draw the handle all the way in toward the sternum. Your upper arms should simulate a rowing motion as they internally rotate. The muscles worked during this motion include:

Biceps and forearms

Trapezius

Deltoids

Latissimus dorsi

Abdominal

Glutes

Quads

Benefits

The indoor rowing machine is great for fitness and full-body toning. It mimics the motion of rowing in the water boasting with many different benefits that include:

Low Impact
Unlike running, rowing is low impact and non-weight bearing. This is because the motion is natural adding minimal stress to your bones and joints. Low impact exercise puts very little to no strain at all on the body and is great for those suffering from knee joint pain, arthritis, and even osteoporosis. In addition, an indoor rowing machine has a much lower risk of injury and makes a great choice for recovery.

Total Body
Many people believe an indoor rowing machine only focuses on strengthening the lower body, but this could not be further from the truth. As mentioned, rowing works 86% of the muscles in your body making it a full-body workout and great for strength training. According to the American Fitness Professionals Association (AFPRA), the rowing stroke is made up of 65% to 75% leg work and 25% to 35% upper bodywork thus making it an incredible total body workout.

Good Cardio
When looking for a good cardio workout or something high intensity, but the treadmill isnt right for you then dont worry. There are other options. Rowing, for example, is an excellent choice for those looking for good cardio exercise. It strengthens the cardiovascular system which includes the heart, blood vessels and blood. It is responsible for transporting oxygen and other nutrients throughout the body. Since rowing is such an intense workout, it requires the heart to work a little harder to transport more blood to the body, therefore boosting your heart rate too.

Calorie Burner
An indoor rowing machine is superior when it comes to weight loss because users are able to vary the intensity of the workout by increasing the resistance of the machine, therefore burning more calories. On average, a rowing machine workout burns 600 calories an hour. That is much more efficient than any other home gym machine on the market. To give you an example, you would have to ride roughly 78 minutes to equal a 60-minute workout on a rowing machine.

In Conclusion
Is a rowing machine a good workout? The answer is yes.

It doesnt matter if you are the strongest bodybuilder on the block or if you are completely new to the exciting world of fitness, indoor rowing machines are great for all fitness levels. They are low impact, offer a total body workout, improve cardiovascular health all while burning tons of calories making an indoor rower an ideal choice for those looking to improve their overall health.
